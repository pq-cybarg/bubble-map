# The US leadership map — what the officeholder data shows (and what it can't)

The leadership directory now holds **13,203 current officeholder records** spanning the federal government, all 50 state governments, and the county tier — every record carrying a name, office, jurisdiction, `as_of` date, and a source URL. This page reads that dataset for structure and pattern, and is deliberate about the difference between what the data documents and what it cannot support.

## Shape of the directory

| Level | Records | Share |
|---|---|---|
| State | 7,914 | 60% |
| County | 3,098 | 23% |
| Federal | 2,191 | 17% |

By branch, the legislative tier dominates (8,000 records — ~7,400 state legislators plus Congress), followed by **law enforcement at 3,120** — now the second-largest category, because the county-sheriff layer was completed for every jurisdiction in the country. Judicial (1,814), executive, military, and independent-agency records fill out the rest.

The county tier is almost entirely the sheriff layer: **3,098 elected sheriff jurisdictions**, verified one by one from official county and sheriff-office sources.

## Finding 1 — American local law enforcement has no single template

The word "county sheriff" hides wide structural variation, and the catalogue documents it precisely:

- **Louisiana** elects sheriffs for 64 **parishes**, not counties; Orleans Parish runs a distinct Criminal Sheriff.
- **Virginia** layers 28 **independent-city** sheriffs on top of its 95 county sheriffs — 123 sheriff jurisdictions in one state — while seven "second-class" cities share a neighboring county's sheriff.
- **Consolidated city-county governments** (Nashville/Davidson, Memphis/Shelby, Philadelphia, Denver, Butte-Silver Bow, Anaconda-Deer Lodge) keep a sheriff even where the county government was merged away.
- **Massachusetts** abolished most county governments but still elects 14 sheriffs, now state officers.
- **Delaware's** three sheriffs hold civil-process powers only — no arrest authority.
- **Connecticut, Alaska, Hawaii, and Rhode Island** elect **no** county sheriff at all; the office was abolished or never existed.

The uniform-looking "3,000 elected sheriffs" is, on inspection, a patchwork of parishes, cities, consolidated governments, limited-power offices, and outright absences.

## Finding 2 — Party affiliation is mostly *unrecorded*, which is itself the finding

Partisan affiliation is publicly recorded for only **943 of 3,098 sheriffs — about 30%**. Among that recorded subset the split is roughly **68% Republican, 24% Democratic, 8% independent**.

That number should not be read as the partisan balance of American sheriffs. It is the balance of the *minority* of seats where a party label was published — skewed by which state associations publish party, and by the many states that run sheriff races on a nonpartisan ballot or through nonpartisan directories. For roughly **70% of sheriffs, partisan affiliation is simply not part of the public record** captured here. The honest conclusion is narrow: where party is recorded, it leans Republican; the national distribution is undetermined by this data.

## Finding 3 — Off-cycle churn: the "four-year elected term" understates turnover

At least **79 sitting sheriffs (2.6%)** reached office **mid-term by appointment or interim succession**, not by winning the seat they now hold. This is a lower bound — it counts only jurisdictions whose records explicitly note the circumstance.

The documented causes cluster into death of a predecessor, resignation, retirement, and — notably — **indictment, suspension, or removal**. Concrete, sourced cases include:

- **San Mateo County, CA** — an appointed sheriff took over after the elected sheriff was removed by the county board.
- **Osceola County, FL** — the governor removed the elected sheriff by executive order.
- **Towns County, GA** — the elected sheriff was suspended by the governor, twice.
- **Williamsburg County, SC** — the elected sheriff was suspended on a public-corruption indictment; the governor appointed an interim.
- **Costilla County, CO** and **City of St. Louis, MO** — resignations mid-term, filled by interim officers.
- A **Mississippi Delta** cluster of counties saw leadership change after a 2025 federal drug-corruption sting.

The pattern is modest in magnitude but real in kind: the top local law-enforcement seat turns over off-cycle — through death, resignation, and scandal — more often than the tidy "elected to a four-year term" model implies, and a meaningful share of that turnover is misconduct-driven.

## Finding 4 — The officeholder substrate intersects the elite-capital network

The leadership map answers *who holds office*. The bubble-map answers *who holds capital and power flows*. They are frequently treated as separate worlds; the data shows they are not.

Named members of the **Dialog** private forum — reconstructed from the June-2026 leak — currently sit in the highest federal offices in this directory: **Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent, Director of National Intelligence Tulsi Gabbard, Senator Ted Cruz, and White House senior advisor Elon Musk** all appear in both datasets (an exact-name lower bound; fuzzy matches such as sitting members of Congress in the leaked roster are additional). More broadly, **69 current officeholders already carry a Persons-of-Interest dossier** in the investigation.

This is the analytic payoff of the cataloguing: the officeholder layer is the substrate on which the capital-and-influence graph rests, and specific individuals bridge the two — holding public office while sitting inside private elite networks. Naming that overlap is documentation of structure, not an imputation of motive: appearing in both a public-office roster and a private-forum directory is a fact about position, not proof of coordination.

## What the data cannot say

- It cannot give a national partisan breakdown of sheriffs (70% unrecorded).
- The appointee/turnover rate is a **lower bound**, not a census — only noted cases are counted.
- Cross-dataset name matches are indicative, not exhaustive; different name forms suppress the true overlap.
- A person appearing across the leadership map, the Dialog roster, and the Persons list is evidence of *position*, not of a unitary plan. The collective structure is not a single mind.

The value of a complete, sourced officeholder directory is not that it proves a thesis. It is that it turns "who runs the country, at every level" from an assumption into a checkable, dated, cited record — and makes the seams (the unrecorded parties, the off-cycle vacancies, the private-forum overlaps) visible enough to examine.
